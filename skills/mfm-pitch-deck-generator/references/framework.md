# Shaan Puri's Pitch Deck Framework — Complete Reference

This is the complete 15-slide framework created by Shaan Puri, who used it to raise $20M+ from VCs. After investing in ~105 startups and reviewing thousands of pitch decks, he distilled the pattern of what works into this specific narrative structure.

**Core philosophy:** "The story is more important than design. Nail the story first, then have a designer style your deck."

## The Narrative Arc

The 15 slides follow a deliberate story structure. Understanding this arc is critical to coaching founders effectively:

**Act 1 — Set the Stage (Slides 1-2):** Who are you, and why should I listen?
**Act 2 — The Problem (Slides 3-5):** What's broken, why current fixes are bad, and what the world should look like instead.
**Act 3 — The Solution (Slides 6-8):** Here's our product, here's how big this could be, and here's proof it's working.
**Act 4 — Why We Win (Slides 9-12):** Our edge, the macro trends, the timing, and why we're the team to do it.
**Act 5 — The Close (Slides 13-15):** What happens next, one more reason to get excited, and how to reach us.

Each act builds on the previous one. If a founder's "problem" slide is weak, the "product" slide won't land. If "traction" is missing, "vision" feels like fantasy. Coach each slide with awareness of how it connects to the slides around it.

## Slide 1: Title
**Purpose:** First impression. The investor should instantly understand what you do.

**Format:**
- Company name / logo
- A one-line subtitle explaining what you do

**Coaching question:** "What is your company name, and how would you explain what you do in a single sentence to someone who knows nothing about your industry?"

**What a strong answer looks like:**
- "Airbnb — we help travelers rent homes instead of hotels"
- Short, clear, no jargon
- Passes the "could someone outside the industry understand this?" test

**What to push back on:**
- Subtitles longer than ~15 words
- Industry jargon or buzzwords ("AI-powered synergistic platform for...")
- Descriptions of the technology rather than the benefit ("We use machine learning to..." → reframe as what the customer gets)

**Example rewrite:**
- Weak: "An AI-driven SaaS platform enabling cross-functional data orchestration for enterprise teams"
- Strong: "We help big companies find answers in their data without needing a data team"

## Slide 2: Credibility
**Purpose:** Build trust before getting into the business. Investors (especially at early stages) are betting on the founder as much as the company.

**Format:**
- A "quick intro" that's really a place to humblebrag
- 3-5 key accomplishments about the founder(s) and/or company

**Coaching question:** "What are your 3-5 most impressive accomplishments — education, previous companies, notable employers, awards, or relevant experiences? Don't be modest. This is your place to brag."

**What a strong answer looks like:**
- "Ex-Facebook engineer, Stanford CS, previously sold a company for $10M"
- "Sold $5M worth of knives door-to-door one summer" (shows hustle, memorable)
- Clear, specific, verifiable accomplishments

**What to push back on:**
- Generic claims without specifics ("experienced entrepreneur" → what specifically did you do?)
- Too many bullet points — pick the 3-5 strongest
- If the credentials are genuinely light, suggest moving this slide to the END of the deck instead (per Shaan's guidance: "If you / your company does not have impressive accomplishments — move this slide to the end")

**Strategic note:** This is one of the few slides where placement is flexible. Strong credentials go near the front to set the stage. Weaker credentials go at the end, after the business story has built the case.

## Slide 3: Problem
**Purpose:** Make the investor feel the pain. If they don't care about the problem, they won't care about the solution.

**Format:**
- "Most [customers] have [this problem]"
- Backed by data or a personal story

**Coaching question:** "What problem does your target customer face? Can you put a number on it (like '74% of Americans have trouble falling asleep') or tell a story from personal experience (like 'I ran a $5M ecommerce store and spent 14 hours a week just analyzing marketing data')?"

**What a strong answer looks like:**
- Specific customer segment, not "everyone"
- Quantified with data OR illustrated with a vivid personal anecdote
- The problem feels urgent, not just annoying

**What to push back on:**
- Vague problems ("People struggle with X" → which people? how many? how badly?)
- Problems that sound like nice-to-haves rather than must-fixes
- Missing data AND missing story — you need at least one

**Example rewrite:**
- Weak: "Small businesses have trouble managing their finances"
- Strong: "78% of small business owners spend 10+ hours per month on bookkeeping — that's time they could be growing their business. Most of them got into entrepreneurship to build something, not to reconcile spreadsheets."

## Slide 4: Current Solutions
**Purpose:** Show that the existing options are bad. This sets up your solution as the obvious next step.

**Format:**
- "How they solve it today..."
- Current solutions that have poor results

**Coaching question:** "How do people currently deal with this problem? What's bad about each option? Make the status quo feel inadequate."

**What a strong answer looks like:**
- 2-3 current alternatives, each with a clear flaw
- The flaws are real, not strawman arguments
- The investor should think "yeah, those options suck"

**What to push back on:**
- Only listing competitors by name without explaining what's wrong
- Making current solutions sound perfectly fine (undermines your case)
- Missing this slide entirely — some founders jump straight from problem to solution

**Shaan's framing:** "Ideally the way it's being solved today isn't great. For example: 'The way most people solve their sleep issues is through prescription drugs that have bad side effects or they simply do nothing.'"

## Slide 5: The "What If"
**Purpose:** Create the emotional bridge between the problem and your product. This is NOT the product slide — it's an aspirational vision of how things should work.

**Format:**
- "What if instead..."
- A simpler, better way described in one sentence

**Coaching question:** "Forget your product for a second. What would the ideal experience look like for your customer? Start with 'What if instead, you could...'"

**What a strong answer looks like:**
- "What if instead, you could eat a sleep gummy and fall asleep within 15 minutes"
- Simple, aspirational, customer-focused
- Makes the listener nod and say "yeah, that would be great"

**What to push back on:**
- Describing the product (save that for the next slide)
- Being too technical ("What if instead, you had an ML pipeline that..." → no, describe the experience)
- Being too vague ("What if instead, things were better?" → how specifically?)

**Why this slide matters:** It builds desire before the reveal. The investor should WANT this to exist before they know you've built it. It's the difference between "let me tell you about my product" and "imagine this... now let me show you we've built it."

## Slide 6: Product
**Purpose:** Reveal your product. After the buildup, this should feel like a satisfying answer.

**Format:**
- "That's us — we do that thing"
- Product screenshots, hero images
- 1-2 key benefits / value props

**Coaching question:** "Show me your product. What does it look like? If you could only highlight 1-2 things about it, what would they be?"

**What a strong answer looks like:**
- A clear product image that looks polished
- 1-2 value props, not a feature list
- Connects directly back to the "what if" from the previous slide

**What to push back on:**
- Listing 5+ features — force them to pick the top 1-2
- No visual — this slide should SHOW the product
- Value props that don't connect to the problem stated earlier

**Shaan's note:** "Show your product. It should look awesome. Use your hero shots, and maybe call out the 1-2 key benefits / value props."

## Slide 7: Vision
**Purpose:** Get the investor excited about the SIZE of the opportunity. This is where you show this could be a huge company.

**Format:**
- "State the dream"
- How this becomes a multi-billion dollar company
- Market size and/or analogy to big companies

**Coaching question:** "How will this be a massive company? Can you frame it as 'We are [something big] for [big industry]'? What's the market size?"

**What a strong answer looks like:**
- "We are Uber for freight trucking. This is a $100B industry that's completely broken."
- "X is a $50B company for websites, but nobody has built X for mobile yet — and mobile is bigger than desktop."
- Analogy + market size = credible ambition

**What to push back on:**
- Vision that's too small for VC ("We'll be a $50M company" — VCs need billion-dollar potential)
- Vision that's too vague ("We'll change the world" — how?)
- Missing market size data
- Analogies that don't hold up ("We're the Uber of toothbrushes" — does that even make sense?)

## Slide 8: Traction
**Purpose:** Prove this isn't just a pitch — it's a real business with real demand. This is the most important slide for separating "talkers" from "walkers."

**Format:**
- "So far, so good..."
- A chart showing growth (revenue, users, waitlist)

**Coaching question:** "What proof do you have that the market wants this? Show me your best number — revenue, user growth, waitlist signups, or anything else that shows momentum."

**What a strong answer looks like:**
- A clear up-and-to-the-right chart
- Revenue is strongest; user growth is good; waitlist growth is acceptable for very early stage
- Specific numbers, not percentages without context

**What to push back on:**
- No metrics at all — even pre-revenue startups should have something (waitlist, LOIs, pilot results, engagement data)
- Vanity metrics ("We have 50K followers" — do they convert?)
- Flat or declining charts presented without context

**Coaching for early-stage founders:** If traction is limited, coach them to present leading indicators: "We launched our waitlist 3 weeks ago and have 2,000 signups without spending on marketing" or "We have LOIs from 5 companies representing $500K in annual revenue."

**Shaan's note:** "Traction is critical. It's the difference between the 'talkers' and 'walkers.' Show that the market actually wants this product."

## Slide 9: Differentiation
**Purpose:** Explain your unfair advantage. Why will YOU win and not someone else?

**Format:**
- "Let me tell you how our approach is different, yet sensible"
- "Others do X, we do Y"

**Coaching question:** "What's your differentiated approach? This can be about your product, business model, or growth strategy. Frame it as: 'Others do X, we do Y — and here's why Y is better.'"

**What a strong answer looks like:**
- A clear "others do X, we do Y" framing
- The differentiation is strategic, not just a feature
- "Yet sensible" — the approach should be different but also logical

**What to push back on:**
- A list of features instead of a strategic insight
- "We're better because we work harder" — that's not a moat
- Differentiation that the investor could poke holes in easily

## Slide 10: Tailwinds
**Purpose:** Show that you're riding a wave. The investor isn't just betting on you — they're betting on an inevitable trend.

**Format:**
- "Let's zoom out — is there a bigger trend here?"
- Macro trend data, industry shifts, behavioral changes

**Coaching question:** "What bigger trend or macro shift are you a part of? Is there an industry trend, regulatory change, or cultural shift that makes this the right time for your company?"

**What a strong answer looks like:**
- A specific, verifiable trend with data
- The trend is clearly connected to why your company will grow
- The investor thinks "even if this team stumbles, the market is going this direction"

**What to push back on:**
- Generic trends ("AI is growing" — be more specific)
- Trends that don't obviously connect to the company
- No data backing the trend claim

**Shaan's note:** "Show that you are riding waves. That way the investor isn't just believing in YOU, they are believing in the WAVE and your ability to surf it."

---

## Slide 11: Why Now
**Purpose:** Explain the timing. Most good ideas come down to timing — why can this work NOW but not 5 years ago?

**Format:**
- "Why NOW?"
- Specific catalyst that makes this possible/inevitable now

**Coaching question:** "Why can this idea work now but couldn't have worked 5 years ago? What specific thing changed — a law, a technology, a behavior shift?"

**What a strong answer looks like:**
- A specific, dateable catalyst: "Obamacare enabled Oscar Health," "ChatGPT made this possible," "remote work shifted behavior"
- The more specific, the better
- The catalyst is external (market/technology/regulation), not internal ("because we started the company")

**What to push back on:**
- "The market is growing" — that's not a why-now, that's a tailwind
- Vague timing arguments
- Internal milestones framed as "why now" ("because we just launched" — investors don't care about your timeline, they care about the market's timeline)

---

## Slide 12: Founder-Market Fit
**Purpose:** Convince the investor that you were put on earth to build this company. Your skills and life experiences prepared you for this.

**Format:**
- "Why are YOU the one to do it?"
- Personal narrative connecting background to the problem

**Coaching question:** "Why are you the right person to build this? How did your life — your career, education, personal experiences — prepare you specifically for this problem?"

**What a strong answer looks like:**
- A clear, compelling narrative arc: "I spent 10 years in freight logistics, saw this problem firsthand, and couldn't find anyone solving it — so I built it"
- The connection feels natural and specific, not forced
- The investor should feel like you have unfair knowledge or passion for this space

**What to push back on:**
- Generic founder stories ("I'm passionate about solving problems" — everyone says that)
- No clear connection between background and company
- Repeating the credibility slide — this slide is about the NARRATIVE, not the resume

**Shaan's note:** "Earlier you hit why you're impressive. Now drive that home. The investor should feel like you were put on earth to do this."

---

## Slide 13: Milestones
**Purpose:** Show what happens with the investment. Make the investor excited about what comes next.

**Format:**
- "Where will we be next year if I give you this money?"
- Specific milestones, not a generic "use of funds" pie chart

**Coaching question:** "If you get this funding, what specific milestones will you hit in the next 12-18 months? Think outcomes, not budget line items."

**What a strong answer looks like:**
- 3-4 specific, measurable milestones: "Hit $2M ARR," "Launch in 3 new markets," "Reach 50K active users"
- Milestones that would clearly make the company more valuable (and make the NEXT round of funding easier)
- The investor should think "if they hit these milestones, this investment will look great"

**What to push back on:**
- Generic "use of funds" pie charts ("40% engineering, 30% marketing..." — boring and uninspiring)
- Milestones that aren't measurable
- Too many milestones (pick the 3-4 most exciting)

**Shaan's note:** "Startups often show the 'use of funds.' Personally, I think it's stronger to highlight the next milestone you're going to hit using this funding instead. This gets people excited."

## Slide 14: Sweeten the Pot
**Purpose:** End on a high note. This is a pro trick — save a piece of good news for the end so the investor leaves feeling excited.

**Format:**
- "Sweeten the pot"
- Good news saved for this section — like dessert

**Coaching question:** "Do you have any good news you've been saving? An unannounced partnership, a new product feature launching soon, a notable advisor who just joined? If not, what are the 3-4 strongest reasons someone should invest?"

**What a strong answer looks like:**
- A genuine surprise: "We just signed a partnership with [big name] that we haven't announced yet"
- Or a strong summary: the 3-4 most compelling investment thesis points restated with punch

**What to push back on:**
- Repeating information from earlier slides without adding anything new
- Weak "good news" that doesn't actually sweeten anything
- Skipping this slide — even if there's no surprise news, a strong closing summary is valuable

**Shaan's note:** "This is a pro-trick. Save a little good news for the end. The reader leaves on a high note."

---

## Slide 15: Call to Action
**Purpose:** Don't forget to tell them how to reach you.

**Format:**
- Contact info (email, phone, calendar link)
- Clear call to action

**Coaching question:** "How should interested investors get in touch with you? Email? Calendar link? What's the best way?"

**What a strong answer looks like:**
- Clean, simple: name, email, and one other contact method
- A clear ask: "We're raising $X. Let's chat."

**What to push back on:**
- Missing contact info (it happens more than you'd think)
- No clear ask or next step
- Too much clutter on the closing slide
